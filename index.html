🌿 Nature & Ephemeris

On the Iberian Peninsula, the second week of March brings an unmistakable acceleration of spring. The Judas tree (Cercis siliquastrum) is beginning to open its magenta flowers directly on bare wood — one of the most striking sights of the Portuguese transition season. In meadows and along roadsides, yellow oxalis, wild marigold, and the first poppies carpet the ground. The air carries a green sweetness of growth that was absent just weeks ago.

Birdlife is intensifying. White storks have long since returned to their nests on chimneys and pylons across the Alentejo. Swallows (Hirundo rustica) are arriving now, their appearance traditionally considered definitive proof that winter has broken. Along the Sado and Tagus estuaries, waders — black-tailed godwits, avocets, dunlins — are feeding heavily, building reserves for their northward migration. The waning gibbous moon, still casting appreciable light in the small hours, keeps nocturnal creatures slightly more cautious; foxes and stone martens hunt in shorter bursts.

In traditional Portuguese agriculture, this is the season for pruning grapevines — the last cuts before the sap rises fully. Fishermen in the Setúbal area are preparing for the sardine season that begins in earnest by late spring. Market gardens around Lisbon are planting broad beans, peas, and early lettuces. The soil, still damp from winter rains, is warm enough to germinate but cool enough to keep weeds at bay — a fleeting equilibrium that experienced farmers know to exploit.

💭 Daily Thought

There is a peculiar honesty to late winter light — it illuminates without flattery, casting long shadows that reveal the true contour of things. We speak of spring as arrival, but what is arriving has been at work invisibly for weeks: roots elongating in darkness, sap pressing upward through cambium, migratory birds already airborne over the Sahara. The visible world lags behind the real one. Perhaps this is always the case — that the most consequential changes in a life are already well underway before we notice them, and what we call a turning point is really the moment we finally consent to see what has long been true.

"The only way to make sense out of change is to plunge into it, move with it, and join the dance." — Alan Watts

Apricity /əˈprɪs.ɪ.ti/

Etymology: From the Latin aprīcitās, from aprīcus ("warmed by the sun, exposed to the sun"), related to aperīre ("to open").

Definition: The warmth of the sun in winter; the particular pleasure of feeling sunlight on one's skin during a cold season.
